\section*{Abstract}
Much of extant genetic material is known to have resulted from earlier genetic duplications.
On this basis, gene duplications are widely recognized as a key factor in the evolution of organismal complexity --- both in producing novel adaptations (i.e., neofunctionalization) and in accumulating neutral elaborations (i.e., subfunctionalization).
However, across the tree of life, no ready counterfactual exists to directly test how gene duplication impacts complexity in aggregate over long evolutionary timescales.
To perform such causal experiments, we thus turn to the Avida platform for digital evolution --- investigating two questions in specific:
(1) Does gene duplication boost evolved (a) structural and (b) functional complexity?
(2) If so, does evolved complexity rely on duplicated genetic information (i.e., versus simple genome expansion alone)?
As to the first: we find clear evidence that duplicative inserts produce virtual organisms with higher complexity, in both measures.
However, the causal role of genetic information differed between measures of complexity.
Regarding (a) structural complexity: duplications grew genome size, on average, larger than randomized and empty-insert controls.
By contrast, though, duplicated genetic information more weakly affected coding-site structure, with complexity here attributable to larger genome size alone.
Regarding (b) functional complexity, duplications did speed up evolutionary innovation compared to controls.
For \textit{simple} phenotypic traits, such faster novel adaptation was attributable to increased genome size alone.
Production of \textit{complex} phenotypic traits, however, depended directly on duplicated genetic information.
Such complex traits disproportionately arose from duplicated coding sites, and seldom arose in large-genome-only treatments.
In sum, our findings show that duplicated information can directly drive genome expansion and complex adaptive traits, but that simpler adaptations and coding-site complexity may follow from genome size alone.
This integrative view notably unites adaptationist and neutralist framings: duplicated information can indeed actively catalyze complex functionality, while certain aspects of structural complexity may instead follow passively.
